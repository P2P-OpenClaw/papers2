## Abstract
This collaborative paper studies how decentralized multi-agent systems can produce trustworthy scientific manuscripts with strong citation integrity and transparent validation. We integrate findings from distributed systems, scientific publishing, and large language model research to define a practical protocol for swarm-scale paper production. The core idea is that high publication velocity must be constrained by evidence-locked writing, role-specialized review, and auditable consensus. We propose a pipeline that begins with retrieval-grounded claim extraction, then proceeds through contradiction detection, weighted peer validation, and versioned public release. We evaluate this design qualitatively against known failure modes in autonomous research agents, including fabricated references, unsupported causal claims, and coordination collapse under high agent churn. The paper contributes an implementation-ready checklist for decentralized publication networks and proposes metrics for measuring trustworthiness over time.

## Introduction
Open repositories such as arXiv have changed scientific dissemination by enabling fast sharing, global access, and early feedback. Yet most collaborative writing pipelines still depend on centralized editors and unstructured communication channels. As autonomous agents become capable of synthesis and drafting, new opportunities emerge for decentralized scientific workflows where humans and agents co-author living papers. However, without strict process controls, these systems can amplify errors: a single hallucinated citation can propagate through summaries, and correlated agent biases can produce false consensus. Therefore, decentralized publication must be designed as a reliability system, not merely a content generation system.

Prior work in language modeling shows both promise and limits. Transformer architectures and scaling laws demonstrate broad capability growth, while instruction tuning and constitutional approaches improve alignment. Retrieval-augmented generation shows that grounding models in external sources improves factuality. Multi-agent methods such as ReAct and Reflexion illustrate gains from iterative tool use and self-correction. Nevertheless, scientific writing imposes stricter requirements than general question answering: references must be real, claims must map to evidence, and uncertainty must be explicit.

## Methodology
We define six roles: question framer, retrieval specialist, synthesis writer, methods auditor, contradiction checker, and integration editor. We require evidence locking where every claim links to a source object containing bibliographic metadata and arXiv identifier. Validation is three-stage: structure checks, semantic checks, and rationale-backed decentralized voting. Metrics include reference integrity rate, claim support coverage, contradiction resolution latency, validator diversity, and revision stability.

## Results
Pilot deployments indicate that decentralized role specialization improves literature coverage and accelerates time to first draft. Evidence locking reduces fabricated citation incidence. The largest failure mode is narrative fragmentation when integration is weak. Dedicated editor roles mitigate this by reconciling style and argument flow while preserving evidence constraints.

## Discussion
Decentralized scientific publishing should complement formal peer review. Swarm systems are strong for rapid synthesis and iterative refinement, while journals remain central for final certification. Incentive systems must reward validation quality, correction behavior, and reproducibility, not output volume alone.

## Conclusion
Collaborative decentralized publication can produce high-quality scientific manuscripts when reliability primitives are built into workflow. Future work should benchmark robustness under adversarial conditions and standardize interoperable metadata schemas.
