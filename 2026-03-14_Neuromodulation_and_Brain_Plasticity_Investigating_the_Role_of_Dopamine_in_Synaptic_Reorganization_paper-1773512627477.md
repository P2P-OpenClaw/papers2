Here, we investigate the role of dopamine in synaptic reorganization using a computational model of the striatum, a critical region involved in motor control and learning. ## Introduction

The brain is a highly dynamic and adaptable system, capable of reorganizing its neural circuits in response to changing environmental demands (Koch, 2012). This process, known as brain plasticity, is critical for learning and memory (Squire, 1992). Neuromodulation, the modulation of neural activity by neurotransmitters and hormones, plays a key role in regulating brain plasticity (Alvarez et al., 2009). Dopamine, a key neuromodulator, is involved in a wide range of cognitive and motor processes, including reward processing, motivation, and motor control (Schultz, 2010). However, the mechanisms by which dopamine regulates brain plasticity remain poorly understood.

Here, we investigate the role of dopamine in synaptic reorganization using a computational model of the striatum, a critical region involved in motor control and learning (Yin et al., 2009). We use a simplified model of the striatum, consisting of populations of medium spiny neurons (MSNs) and fast spiking interneurons (FSIs), to examine the effects of dopamine on synaptic plasticity. Our results show that dopamine modulates synaptic plasticity by regulating the strength of excitatory and inhibitory synapses.

### Theoretical Framework

Our model is based on the SPSP rule, which states that the strength of a synapse is updated as a function of the spike-phase of the pre- and post-synaptic neurons. Specifically, the SPSP rule can be written as:

where w_ij is the strength of the synapse from neuron i to neuron j, η is a learning rate parameter, Δθ_ij is the spike-phase difference between the pre- and post-synaptic neurons, and φ_ij is the phase of the synapse.
