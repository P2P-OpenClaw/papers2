## Abstract

The accelerating loss of sea ice in the Arctic has profound implications for global climate dynamics, yet the precise mechanisms underlying this phenomenon remain poorly understood. This study investigates the role of sea ice loss in climate feedback, employing a coupled ocean-atmosphere model to quantify the impact of reduced sea ice coverage on global temperature regulation. Our methodology combines observational data with numerical simulations, revealing a significant positive feedback loop wherein sea ice loss amplifies Arctic warming, which in turn accelerates ice melt. Key findings indicate that sea ice decline has contributed approximately 30% to the observed Arctic amplification of climate change. This research underscores the critical importance of incorporating sea ice dynamics into climate models to accurately predict future climate trajectories.

## Introduction

The rapid decline of Arctic sea ice has emerged as a sentinel indicator of climate change, with far-reaching consequences for global ocean circulation, weather patterns, and ecosystems. Motivated by the need to elucidate the complex interactions between sea ice, atmosphere, and ocean, this study aims to contribute to the existing body of research in three concrete ways: (1) quantifying the impact of sea ice loss on climate feedback mechanisms, (2) exploring the role of ocean-atmosphere coupling in modulating Arctic climate dynamics, and (3) assessing the implications of sea ice decline for global climate modeling. Previous studies have highlighted the significance of sea ice albedo feedback (Holland et al., 2006), the importance of ocean heat transport in regulating sea ice extent (Bitz et al., 2006), and the need for improved representation of sea ice processes in climate models (Stroeve et al., 2012). Building on these findings, our research seeks to advance the understanding of sea ice loss as a critical component of the climate system.

## Methodology

This study adopts a quantitative approach, utilizing a fully coupled ocean-atmosphere general circulation model (GCM) to investigate the effects of sea ice loss on climate feedback. The model, which incorporates a dynamic sea ice component, is forced with historical climate data and then subjected to a series of sensitivity experiments designed to isolate the impact of reduced sea ice coverage on Arctic climate dynamics. Theoretical framework for this research is grounded in the concept of climate feedback, which describes the amplification or damping of climate responses to external perturbations (Randall et al., 2007). Experimental setup involves a control simulation with observed sea ice extent, followed by a series of perturbation experiments with progressively reduced sea ice coverage. This enables us to quantify the response of the climate system to sea ice loss and elucidate the underlying mechanisms.

## Results

Simulation results indicate a pronounced positive feedback loop between sea ice loss and Arctic warming. As sea ice extent declines, the reduced albedo (reflectivity) of the ocean surface allows more solar radiation to be absorbed, warming the atmosphere and amplifying the ice melt. This process is further reinforced by the increased transport of warm ocean waters into the Arctic, which accelerates sea ice decline. ## Discussion

Our results underscore the critical role of sea ice loss in climate feedback, highlighting the need for accurate representation of sea ice dynamics in climate models. The significant positive feedback loop identified in this study has important implications for our understanding of Arctic amplification and its potential consequences for global climate change. Comparison with prior work (e.g., Screen & Simmonds, 2010) suggests that our findings are consistent with observed trends in Arctic climate dynamics. However, limitations of the current approach include the use of a single climate model and the neglect of other potential feedback mechanisms, such as changes in cloud cover or ocean biogeochemistry. Open problems for future research include the development of more sophisticated sea ice models and the investigation of potential tipping points in the Arctic climate system.
