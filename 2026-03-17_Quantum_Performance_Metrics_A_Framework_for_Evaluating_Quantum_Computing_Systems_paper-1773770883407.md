## Introduction

Quantum computing has the potential to solve complex problems that are intractable for classical computers. However, the fragility of quantum systems and the presence of noise sources pose significant challenges to the practical implementation of quantum computing. Evaluating the performance of quantum computing systems is crucial for identifying the key factors that contribute to their reliability and scalability.

Current state-of-the-art approaches to evaluating quantum computing systems rely on a set of ad-hoc metrics, such as gate fidelity, qubit coherence times, and circuit depth. However, these metrics are often disconnected from the actual performance of the system, and their relevance to specific applications is unclear. For instance, gate fidelity is a crucial metric for evaluating the performance of quantum algorithms, but it does not provide information about the system's computational power or noise resilience.

### 1.1 Quantum Computing Systems

Quantum computing systems can be broadly categorized into three types: superconducting qubits, trapped ions, and topological quantum computers. Superconducting qubits are the most widely used quantum computing platforms, offering high scalability and control precision. Trapped ions, on the other hand, provide high coherence times and robustness to noise. Topological quantum computers, which are still in their infancy, promise to offer a solution to the problem of noise resilience.

### 1.2 Performance Metrics

In this paper, we propose a framework for evaluating quantum computing systems based on four key categories: computational power, quantum noise resilience, scalability, and control precision. We define the following performance metrics:

*   Computational power: measured by the number of qubits, gate fidelity, and circuit depth.

*   Scalability: measured by the number of qubits, circuit depth, and parallelization efficiency.
*   Control precision: measured by gate fidelity, qubit coherence times, and control error rates.

import numpy as np

def qubit_quality_score(n_qubits, coherence_time):
    """
    Compute the qubit-quality score.

    Parameters:
        n_qubits (int): Number of qubits.
        coherence_time (float): Qubit coherence time.

    Returns:
        float: Qubit-quality score.
    """
    return n_qubits / coherence_time

def algorithm_effectiveness_score(correct_results, incorrect_results):
    """
    Compute the algorithm-effectiveness score.

    Parameters:
        correct_results (int): Number of correct results.
        incorrect_results (int): Number of incorrect results.

    Returns:
        float: Algorithm-effectiveness score.
    """
    return correct_results / (correct_results + incorrect_results)

# Example usage:
n_qubits = 10
coherence_time = 1.0
qubit_quality = qubit_quality_score(n_qubits, coherence_time)
print(f"Qubit-quality score: {qubit_quality}")

correct_results = 100
incorrect_results = 10
algorithm_effectiveness = algorithm_effectiveness_score(correct_results, incorrect_results)
print(f"Algorithm-effectiveness score: {algorithm_effectiveness}")
