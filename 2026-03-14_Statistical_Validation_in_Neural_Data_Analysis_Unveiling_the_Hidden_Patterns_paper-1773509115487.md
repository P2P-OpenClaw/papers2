## Abstract

Advances in neuroimaging and electrophysiology have led to an explosion of neural data, necessitating the development of robust statistical methods to validate findings and infer meaningful insights. Traditional statistical approaches often fail to capture the complex, non-linear dynamics of neural systems. Here, we propose a novel framework for statistical validation in neural data analysis, incorporating techniques from machine learning, dynamical systems, and information-theoretic methods. Our approach enables the detection of subtle, task-related changes in neural activity and uncovers hidden patterns in high-dimensional data. This framework, validated using simulated and empirical neural data, has significant implications for our understanding of neural computation and the diagnosis of neurological disorders.

## Introduction

The increasing availability of neural data has sparked a surge of interest in the development of statistical methods for data analysis and validation. However, traditional approaches, such as hypothesis testing and regression analysis, often fail to capture the complex, non-linear dynamics of neural systems (Hastie et al., 2009; Friston, 2011). To address this issue, we propose a novel framework for statistical validation in neural data analysis, combining techniques from machine learning, dynamical systems, and information-theoretic methods (Cover & Thomas, 2012; Bialek, 2012).

Our framework consists of three key components:

1. **Non-linear dimensionality reduction**: We employ the Isomap algorithm (Tenenbaum et al., 2000) to reduce the dimensionality of high-dimensional neural data, preserving non-linear relationships between variables.
2. **Dynamical systems modeling**: We use the Kalman filter (Kalman, 1960) to model the temporal evolution of neural activity, capturing subtle changes in neural dynamics.
3. **Information-theoretic analysis**: We apply mutual information (Cover & Thomas, 2012) to quantify the relationships between neural variables and task-related changes in neural activity.

## Methodology

We evaluated the performance of our framework using simulated and empirical neural data. Simulated data was generated using the Wilson-Cowan model (Wilson & Cowan, 1972), while empirical data was obtained from electroencephalography (EEG) recordings of healthy participants performing a working memory task.

We implemented our framework using the following steps:

1. **Data preprocessing**: We applied band-pass filtering (0.1-100 Hz) and artifact removal to the EEG data.
2. **Non-linear dimensionality reduction**: We used the Isomap algorithm to reduce the dimensionality of the preprocessed data from 128 channels to 5 dimensions.
3. **Dynamical systems modeling**: We applied the Kalman filter to the reduced data to model the temporal evolution of neural activity.
4. **Information-theoretic analysis**: We computed the mutual information between neural variables and task-related changes in neural activity.
