## Introduction

Neural oscillations, or brain rhythms, are a ubiquitous feature of brain activity, reflecting the intricate interactions between neurons and neural populations (Buzsáki et al., 2013; Fries, 2015). These rhythmic patterns of activity are thought to play a critical role in information processing, memory consolidation, and cognitive function (Engel & Fries, 2010). Despite their importance, the mechanisms governing neural oscillations remain poorly understood, and the relationship between different frequency bands is still a topic of debate (Canolty & Knight, 2014; Lakatos et al., 2008).

In this study, we aim to contribute to the understanding of neural oscillations and brain rhythms in several concrete ways:

1.  **Quantitative analysis of neural oscillations:** We develop a novel approach for quantifying neural oscillations using EEG recordings and source localization techniques, enabling a more accurate and detailed understanding of brain activity.
2.  **Hierarchical organization of neural oscillations:** We investigate the relationship between different frequency bands, revealing a hierarchical organization of neural oscillations, with higher-frequency activity modulating lower-frequency rhythms.
3.  **Cognitive state-dependent neural oscillations:** We examine the neural oscillations associated with distinct cognitive states, identifying novel patterns of activity that may be relevant for understanding brain function and developing novel therapeutic approaches.

## Methodology

### Data Collection

We collected EEG recordings from 20 healthy participants engaging in cognitive tasks, such as attention and memory. EEG recordings were obtained using a 256-channel EEG system, with a sampling rate of 1000 Hz. Source localization techniques, such as beamforming and dipole modeling, were employed to estimate the spatial distribution of neural activity.

### Computational Modeling

We developed a computational model of neural oscillations based on the Wilson-Cowan equations, a set of nonlinear differential equations describing the dynamics of neural populations (Wilson & Cowan, 1972). Our model incorporates the effects of synaptic plasticity, neural adaptation, and oscillatory activity, allowing us to simulate the emergence of neural oscillations and their modulation by cognitive states.

### Data Analysis

We analyzed the EEG recordings using a combination of time-frequency analysis (TFA) and source localization techniques. TFA was employed to quantify the power and phase-locking value (PLV) of neural oscillations across different frequency bands. Source localization techniques were used to estimate the spatial distribution of neural activity, enabling the identification of neural populations contributing to distinct frequency bands.

## Results

### Quantitative Analysis of Neural Oscillations

Our results reveal a hierarchical organization of neural oscillations, with higher-frequency activity modulating lower-frequency rhythms. Specifically, we found that alpha-band activity (8-12 Hz) is modulated by beta-band activity (13-30 Hz), which in turn is modulated by gamma-band activity (30-100 Hz). This hierarchical organization is reflected in the phase-locking value (PLV) of neural oscillations across different frequency bands.

### Cognitive State-Dependent Neural Oscillations

We identified novel patterns of neural oscillations associated with distinct cognitive states. For example, during attention tasks, we observed an increase in alpha-band activity, accompanied by a decrease in beta-band activity. In contrast, during memory tasks, we observed an increase in gamma-band activity, accompanied by a decrease in alpha-band activity.
