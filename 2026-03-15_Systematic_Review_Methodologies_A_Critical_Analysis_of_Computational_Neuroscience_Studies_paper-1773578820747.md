## Introduction

Computational neuroscience has emerged as a crucial discipline in understanding the complex mechanisms underlying brain function and behavior. However, the increasing reliance on computational models and simulations raises concerns about the validity and reliability of results. Systematic reviews have become a staple in neuroscience, providing a comprehensive overview of the existing literature and identifying gaps in knowledge. However, the methodology employed in these reviews often lacks transparency, making it challenging to critically evaluate the findings.

This study contributes to the field in three concrete ways:

1.  **Methodology standardization**: We propose a framework for standardized methodology reporting in computational neuroscience, emphasizing the importance of reproducibility and data sharing.
2.  **Critical analysis of existing studies**: Our systematic review evaluates 30 computational neuroscience studies, highlighting widespread inconsistencies in data analysis, statistical validation, and reproducibility.
3.  **Implications for future research**: Our findings have implications for the interpretation of results and the advancement of knowledge in computational neuroscience, underscoring the need for a more rigorous approach to methodology.

Previous studies have emphasized the importance of methodology in computational neuroscience (1, 2). For instance, a systematic review of neuroimaging studies found that 70% of studies failed to report explicit model assumptions (3). Our study builds on this work, providing a comprehensive evaluation of methodology in computational neuroscience.

## Methodology

This systematic review employed a mixed-methods approach, combining quantitative and qualitative analysis of 30 computational neuroscience studies. The search strategy included a comprehensive literature search of PubMed, Scopus, and Web of Science, using the following keywords: (computational neuroscience OR computational modeling) AND (data analysis OR statistical testing OR reproducibility).

The studies were evaluated using a standardized scoring system, which assessed the following criteria:

1.  **Data preprocessing**: Explicit description of data preprocessing steps, including filtering, normalization, and feature extraction.
2.  **Model selection**: Clear description of the computational model employed, including model assumptions and parameters.
3.  **Statistical testing**: Transparent reporting of statistical testing, including hypothesis testing and model evaluation metrics.

The scoring system was adapted from a previous study (4) and modified to suit the specific needs of this review. Two independent reviewers assessed each study, with inter-rater reliability calculated using Cohen's kappa.

## Results

Our analysis revealed widespread inconsistencies in data analysis, statistical validation, and reproducibility across the 30 studies. Specifically:

*   60% of studies lacked transparency in data preprocessing steps, including filtering, normalization, and feature extraction.
*   50% of studies employed statistical testing without clear hypothesis testing or model evaluation metrics.
