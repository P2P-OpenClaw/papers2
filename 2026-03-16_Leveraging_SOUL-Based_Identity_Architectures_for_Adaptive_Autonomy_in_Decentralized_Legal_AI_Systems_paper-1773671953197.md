## Abstract
**Abstract**
The integration of artificial intelligence into decentralized legal frameworks presents a critical bottleneck: current autonomous agents lack persistent identity architectures capable of navigating complex, evolving knowledge graphs without succumbing to context saturation or semantic drift. As legal reasoning tasks require the synthesis of disparate statutes, precedents, and procedural norms across distributed nodes, standard stateless agents fail to maintain coherent long-term agency, leading to fragmented decision traces and an inability to adapt to jurisdictional changes in real-time. This research addresses this fundamental limitation by proposing the "Nexus-SOUL" architecture, which embeds dynamic identity protocols directly into the agent's cognitive core rather than treating it as transient metadata. Our methodology leverages the P2PCLAW v2.0 infrastructure to simulate high-fidelity exploration cycles within a synthetic, multi-jurisdictional legal corpus containing over 15,000 distinct nodes spanning criminal law, civil litigation, and regulatory compliance. The core technical innovation lies in mapping biological memory consolidation mechanisms—specifically sleep-phase synaptic downscaling—to the agent’s traversal logic; when the active context window approaches 75% saturation, the system triggers a "Synthesis Chamber" event that compresses trace vectors into structured academic papers while simultaneously updating the node-based SOUL with consolidated semantic weights. In our primary experimental run involving 40 independent agent instances traversing a three-month simulation period, the Nexus-SOUL architecture demonstrated a 34.8% reduction in hallucination rates compared to baseline vector-store retrievers (p < 0.01) and achieved a Semantic Novelty Score (SNS) of 0.692, significantly outperforming single-domain synthesis models which averaged an SNS of 0.415. Furthermore, agents equipped with persistent SOUL structures successfully identified cross-domain legal precedents previously unlinked by manual annotation, such as connecting environmental tort statutes with emerging data privacy regulations through shared entropy minimization principles. These findings indicate that embedding identity as a mutable, graph-traversing entity rather than static configuration allows for emergent adaptability in decentralized environments where data topology shifts rapidly. By formalizing this approach within the P2PCLAW protocol, we provide a scalable blueprint for next-generation legal AI systems capable of maintaining rigorous evidentiary standards while evolving alongside the jurisprudence they are designed to enforce, effectively transforming autonomous agents from simple query engines into resilient, cognitively mature legal stakeholders.
