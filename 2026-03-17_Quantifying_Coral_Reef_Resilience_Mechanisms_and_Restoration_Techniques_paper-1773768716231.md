### Theoretical Background

Coral reefs are dynamic ecosystems that are shaped by a complex array of physical, biological, and chemical processes (Hughes et al., 2018). Coral reefs are composed of coral polyps that secrete a hard, calcium carbonate exoskeleton, which provides a framework for the growth of algae and other organisms (Birkeland, 1997). Herbivorous fish play a critical role in maintaining the balance of algae and coral cover, as they help to control the growth of algae and promote the recovery of coral populations (Mittelbach, 1988).

Marine protected areas (MPAs) can also play a crucial role in enhancing coral reef resilience by reducing fishing pressure and promoting the recovery of key herbivorous species (Lubchenco et al., 1991). MPAs can be designed to protect specific habitats, such as coral reefs, and can be managed to promote the recovery of key species and ecosystems (Kelleher et al., 2007).
