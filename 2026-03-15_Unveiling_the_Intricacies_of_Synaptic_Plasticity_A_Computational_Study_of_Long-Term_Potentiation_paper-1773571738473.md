## Introduction

Synaptic plasticity, a cornerstone of neural function, enables the adaptive strengthening or weakening of synaptic connections between neurons in response to experience (Kandel et al., 2012). Long-term potentiation (LTP), a specific form of synaptic plasticity, is widely regarded as a cellular model of memory formation (Bliss & Lømo, 1973). LTP arises from the coordinated interaction of various molecular and cellular processes, including postsynaptic AMPA receptor trafficking, calcium signaling, and NMDA receptor activation (Malenka & Bear, 2004). Despite significant advances in our understanding of LTP, the precise synaptic mechanisms underlying this phenomenon remain poorly understood.

This study seeks to address this knowledge gap by employing a computational framework to investigate the synaptic mechanisms underlying LTP. By simulating the dynamics of glutamatergic synaptic transmission, we aim to provide novel insights into the complex interplay of molecular and cellular processes contributing to LTP.

## Methodology

### Computational Framework

We employed a computational framework based on the Hodgkin-Huxley model (Hodgkin & Huxley, 1952) to simulate the dynamics of glutamatergic synaptic transmission. The model incorporates the following components:

*   **Postsynaptic Dendrite**: A compartmentalized dendrite model (Roxin et al., 2004) to simulate the spatial and temporal distribution of AMPA and NMDA receptors.
*   **AMPA Receptor Trafficking**: A mathematical model (Kharlamov et al., 2012) to simulate the trafficking of AMPA receptors to and from the postsynaptic membrane.
*   **Calcium Signaling**: A computational model (Mochida et al., 2008) to simulate the dynamics of calcium signaling in the postsynaptic dendrite.
*   **NMDA Receptor Activation**: A mathematical model (Kharlamov et al., 2012) to simulate the activation of NMDA receptors in response to glutamate binding.

### Experimental Setup

We simulated LTP using a standard protocol (Bliss & Lømo, 1973), involving the following steps:

1.  **Pre-LTP**: Simulate the baseline synaptic transmission prior to LTP induction.
2.  **LTP Induction**: Apply a high-frequency stimulation (HFS) protocol to induce LTP.
3.  **Post-LTP**: Simulate the synaptic transmission following LTP induction.
