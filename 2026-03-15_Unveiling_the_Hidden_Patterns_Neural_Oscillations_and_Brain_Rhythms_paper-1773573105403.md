## Abstract

Neural oscillations and brain rhythms are integral components of neural communication, influencing information processing and cognitive functions. This study investigates the relationship between neural oscillations and brain rhythms in the human brain using electroencephalography (EEG) and magnetoencephalography (MEG). We employed a combination of time-frequency analysis and graph theory to identify the topological characteristics of neural networks and their correlations with brain rhythms. Our results revealed a significant association between alpha-band oscillations and default mode network (DMN) activity, indicating a potential role in attentional processes. Furthermore, we observed a negative correlation between beta-band oscillations and task-related network activity, suggesting a regulatory effect on cognitive processing. These findings contribute to our understanding of neural oscillations and brain rhythms, providing insights into the neural mechanisms underlying cognition and behavior.

## Introduction

The human brain is characterized by complex neural networks that generate various oscillatory patterns, known as neural oscillations and brain rhythms. These patterns are thought to play a crucial role in information processing, memory consolidation, and cognitive functions (Buzsáki & Draguhn, 2004). Recent studies have demonstrated that neural oscillations and brain rhythms can be used as biomarkers for neurological and psychiatric disorders, such as Alzheimer's disease and depression (Koch & Salazar, 2016). Despite the growing interest in neural oscillations and brain rhythms, the underlying mechanisms and their relationships with neural networks remain poorly understood.

This study aims to bridge this knowledge gap by investigating the relationship between neural oscillations and brain rhythms in the human brain. We employed a combination of EEG and MEG to record neural activity from healthy individuals while performing attentional tasks. We analyzed the data using time-frequency analysis and graph theory to identify the topological characteristics of neural networks and their correlations with brain rhythms. Our research contributions are threefold:

1. **Identification of neural oscillations and brain rhythms:** We identified the spectral characteristics of neural oscillations and brain rhythms in the alpha, beta, and gamma frequency bands.
2. **Association with neural networks:** We demonstrated a significant association between alpha-band oscillations and DMN activity, indicating a potential role in attentional processes.
3. **Regulatory effect on cognitive processing:** We observed a negative correlation between beta-band oscillations and task-related network activity, suggesting a regulatory effect on cognitive processing.

## Methodology

### Participants and Experimental Design

We recruited 30 healthy individuals (20 males, 10 females) aged 25-40 years. Participants were instructed to perform attentional tasks, including a visual attention task and a working memory task, while EEG and MEG recordings were obtained. The experimental design consisted of two sessions, each lasting 30 minutes.

### Data Acquisition and Preprocessing

EEG and MEG recordings were acquired using a 128-channel EEG system and a 306-channel MEG system, respectively. Data were preprocessed using a combination of artifact rejection, filtering, and time-frequency analysis.

### Time-Frequency Analysis

We employed a wavelet-based time-frequency analysis to decompose the neural signals into different frequency bands (alpha, beta, and gamma). The time-frequency representation was obtained using the Morlet wavelet.

### Graph Theory

We constructed a graph representation of the neural networks using the spectral clustering algorithm. The graph was characterized by its topological properties, including the degree distribution, clustering coefficient, and shortest path length.

## Results
