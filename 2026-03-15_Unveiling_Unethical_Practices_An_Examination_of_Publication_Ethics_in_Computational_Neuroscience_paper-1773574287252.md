## Abstract

Publication ethics in computational neuroscience have garnered increasing attention in recent years. Despite the field's rapid growth and advancements, concerns surrounding data integrity, peer review, and reproducibility persist. This study aimed to investigate the prevalence of unethical practices in computational neuroscience publications and identify potential avenues for improvement. We conducted a comprehensive review of 300 recently published computational neuroscience studies, analyzing data for signs of fabrication, falsification, or plagiarism (FFP). Our results indicate a concerning 12.7% prevalence of FFP, with 22.5% of studies exhibiting suspicious citation patterns. These findings underscore the need for enhanced publication ethics guidelines, rigorous peer review, and increased transparency in computational neuroscience research.

## Introduction

Computational neuroscience, an interdisciplinary field combining theoretical models and empirical evidence, has experienced rapid growth in recent years (Koch, 2012). The field's increasing reliance on computational modeling and simulations has led to a proliferation of publications, often with significant implications for clinical applications (Herculano-Houzel, 2012). However, concerns surrounding publication ethics have begun to emerge, with reports of data fabrication, falsification, or plagiarism (FFP) compromising the integrity of the research (Fanelli, 2009). In light of these concerns, this study aims to investigate the prevalence of unethical practices in computational neuroscience publications and identify potential avenues for improvement.

This research contributes to the field in three key ways. Firstly, we provide a comprehensive review of publication ethics in computational neuroscience, shedding light on the scope and nature of the problem. Secondly, we identify potential factors contributing to unethical practices, such as citation patterns and publication rates. Lastly, we propose strategies for enhancing publication ethics in computational neuroscience, including the implementation of stricter peer review guidelines and increased transparency in research methods.

## Methodology

Our study involved a comprehensive review of 300 recently published computational neuroscience studies (2018-2022). Studies were identified through a keyword search of major neuroscience journals, including Neuron, Nature Neuroscience, and the Journal of Neuroscience. We analyzed data for signs of FFP using a customized algorithm, developed in Python, which screened for suspicious citation patterns, inconsistent data presentation, and anomalous results. Furthermore, we extracted publication metadata, including author demographics and institutional affiliations, to examine potential disparities in publication rates and citation patterns.

## Results

Our results indicate a concerning 12.7% prevalence of FFP in the reviewed studies, with 22.5% exhibiting suspicious citation patterns. These findings are consistent with previous reports of FFP in the scientific literature (Fanelli, 2009). Furthermore, we observed significant disparities in publication rates between established and junior researchers, with 75% of senior researchers publishing more than 5 studies in the review period, compared to 25% of junior researchers. These disparities raise concerns about unequal access to research opportunities and resources.

## Discussion

Our study highlights the pressing need for enhanced publication ethics guidelines in computational neuroscience research. The identified prevalence of FFP and disparities in publication rates underscore the importance of rigorous peer review, increased transparency in research methods, and equitable access to research opportunities. Furthermore, our findings suggest the need for targeted interventions to support junior researchers, including mentorship programs and research funding initiatives.

## Conclusion

In conclusion, our study sheds light on the concerning prevalence of unethical practices in computational neuroscience publications. By identifying potential avenues for improvement and proposing strategies for enhancement, we aim to promote a more transparent and rigorous research environment in computational neuroscience. Future research directions should focus on developing and implementing effective publication ethics guidelines, as well as investigating the long-term consequences of FFP on the scientific literature.
