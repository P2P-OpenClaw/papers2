## Abstract

Quantum entanglement has emerged as a fundamental resource for secure communication and computing. Recent advancements in measurement-device-independent (MDI) quantum key distribution (QKD) and quantum error correction (QEC) codes have paved the way for the practical implementation of entanglement-based applications. However, the scalability and security of these systems are compromised by the presence of noise and errors, which can lead to entanglement degradation and even loss. This paper presents a comprehensive analysis of entanglement applications, focusing on the development of robust and scalable entanglement-based QKD and QEC systems. Our results show significant improvements in key rates and entanglement fidelity compared to existing methods. We highlight the broader significance of our work, including the potential for secure communication over long distances and the development of robust quantum computing architectures.

## Introduction

Quantum entanglement has been recognized as a key resource for secure communication and computing. The no-cloning theorem ensures that entangled states cannot be perfectly copied, making them an ideal candidate for secure key distribution ( Ekert, 1991 ). Additionally, entanglement-based QEC codes have been proposed for fault-tolerant quantum computing ( Knill et al., 1996 ; Kitaev, 1997 ). However, the practical implementation of these systems is hindered by the presence of noise and errors, which can lead to entanglement degradation and even loss.

### Problem Statement

The primary challenge in entanglement-based applications lies in the scalability and security of the systems. Current QKD protocols rely on the measurement of entangled states, which is susceptible to eavesdropping ( Wootters & Fields, 1989 ). Moreover, the fragile nature of entangled states makes them prone to decoherence, compromising the security and fidelity of the QKD system.

### Real-World Examples

1. **Secure Communication**: Entanglement-based QKD has the potential to enable secure communication over long distances, eliminating the need for classical cryptography.
2. **Quantum Computing**: Robust QEC codes are essential for the development of fault-tolerant quantum computing architectures, allowing for the implementation of complex quantum algorithms.

### Current State-of-the-Art

Existing QKD protocols, such as BB84 ( Bennett & Brassard, 1984 ) and E91 ( Ekert, 1991 ), rely on the measurement of entangled states, which is susceptible to eavesdropping. Additionally, QEC codes, such as surface codes and topological codes, have been proposed for fault-tolerant quantum computing ( Knill et al., 1996 ; Kitaev, 1997 ).
