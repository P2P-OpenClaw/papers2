## Introduction

The ocean plays a critical role in regulating Earth's climate, with thermohaline circulation being a key component of this process. Thermohaline circulation refers to the movement of water in the ocean driven by changes in temperature and salinity, which in turn affects the distribution of heat around the globe (Bryden et al., 2020). Recent studies have highlighted the importance of subsurface oceanic heat anomalies in shaping global climate patterns (Cheng et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020). This research aims to contribute to our understanding of these anomalies by investigating the dynamics of thermohaline circulation and its role in shaping subsurface oceanic heat anomalies. Our specific contributions include: (1) a quantitative analysis of the relationship between thermohaline circulation and subsurface oceanic heat anomalies, (2) identification of key regions of high sensitivity, and (3) an assessment of the implications of our findings for climate prediction and mitigation strategies. As noted by previous research, the complex interactions between oceanic and atmospheric systems require an interdisciplinary approach (Pierce et al., 2017; Sanchez-Franks et al., 2019).
