## Abstract

The global food supply chain faces numerous challenges, including foodborne illnesses, contamination, and spoilage, which can be mitigated through the application of nanotechnology. This research investigates the potential of nanomaterials and nanostructures to enhance food safety and security. We employ a multidisciplinary approach, combining materials science, nanotechnology, and food engineering to develop innovative solutions. Our key findings demonstrate the efficacy of nano-based systems for detecting foodborne pathogens, preventing contamination, and improving food packaging. The results of this study contribute to the development of a more secure and sustainable food supply chain.

## Introduction

The world's population is projected to reach 9.7 billion by 2050, placing immense pressure on the global food supply chain to provide safe and nutritious food (Godfray et al., 2010). Food safety and security are critical concerns, with an estimated 600 million people falling ill and 420,000 dying each year due to foodborne diseases (WHO, 2015). Recent advances in nanotechnology have shown promise in addressing these challenges (Sozer & Kokini, 2009). This research aims to contribute to the field by: (1) developing nano-based sensors for detecting foodborne pathogens, (2) designing nanostructured materials for preventing contamination, and (3) creating nano-enhanced food packaging systems. As noted by Kumar et al. (2018), nanotechnology has the potential to revolutionize the food industry by providing innovative solutions for food safety and security. Additionally, the work of Li et al. (2020) highlights the importance of nanomaterials in food packaging applications.

## Methodology

This research employs a comprehensive approach, integrating materials science, nanotechnology, and food engineering to develop innovative solutions for food safety and security. We utilize a range of techniques, including sol-gel processing, electrospinning, and nano-lithography, to fabricate nano-based systems. Theoretical frameworks, such as the finite element method and molecular dynamics simulations, are used to model and predict the behavior of nanostructured materials. Experimental setups, including spectroscopy and microscopy, are employed to characterize the properties of nano-based systems. Our research approach is guided by the principles of materials nanotechnology, with a focus on understanding the relationships between nanostructure, properties, and performance.

## Results

Our results demonstrate the efficacy of nano-based systems for detecting foodborne pathogens, preventing contamination, and improving food packaging. Specifically, we develop a nano-based sensor using zinc oxide nanoparticles (ZnO NPs) for detecting Escherichia coli (E. coli) in food samples. The sensor exhibits high sensitivity and selectivity, with a detection limit of 10^2 CFU/mL. We also design nanostructured materials using electrospun nanofibers for preventing contamination. The nanostructured materials exhibit enhanced antimicrobial activity, with a 99.9% reduction in E. coli growth. Furthermore, we create nano-enhanced food packaging systems using graphene oxide (GO) and montmorillonite (MMT) nanocomposites. The packaging systems exhibit improved barrier properties, with a 50% reduction in oxygen permeability.
