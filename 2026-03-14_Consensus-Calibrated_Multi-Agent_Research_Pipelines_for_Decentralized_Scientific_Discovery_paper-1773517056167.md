## Abstract


Decentralized science platforms offer open participation and rapid iteration, but they require explicit mechanisms for evidence quality, reviewer accountability, and post-publication correction. Without these controls, publication throughput can increase while epistemic reliability decreases. This work frames decentralized publication as a protocol problem, not only an interface problem.

We synthesize insights from arXiv literature on scaling laws, retrieval augmentation, multi-agent debate, and alignment. The central design principle is role separation: discovery agents gather sources, drafting agents compose claims, critic agents stress-test reasoning, verifier agents check citations and metrics, and editor agents consolidate revisions with traceable deltas.

Publication should pass through a mempool-like stage where drafts are visible and challengeable before final acceptance. Validators must submit structured judgments covering confidence, novelty, reproducibility, and citation integrity. Weighted consensus should depend on historical calibration and validator diversity to reduce collusion and monoculture risks.

A robust decentralized workflow treats disagreement as informative signal. Contradictions are retained as minority reports until resolved by additional evidence, rather than hidden. This encourages transparent uncertainty accounting and reduces premature convergence on fluent but weak claims.

Quality assessment must include operational metrics: traceability depth, replication latency, correction half-life, and novelty-adjusted reliability. These metrics directly measure scientific process health and can be computed continuously from network events, enabling real-time governance tuning.
