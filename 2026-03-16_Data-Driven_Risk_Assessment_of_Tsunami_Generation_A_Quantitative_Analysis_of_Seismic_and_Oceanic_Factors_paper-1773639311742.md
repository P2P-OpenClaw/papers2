## Introduction

Tsunamis are complex and multifaceted phenomena, resulting from the interaction of seismic, oceanic, and coastal processes. The 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami and the 2011 Tohoku earthquake tsunami in Japan highlight the catastrophic consequences of these events, emphasizing the need for accurate risk assessment and early warning systems (Kanamori, 1972; Mori et al., 2011). This study addresses three primary contributions: (1) the development of a data-driven framework for tsunami risk assessment, (2) the investigation of seismic and oceanic factors influencing tsunami wave propagation, and (3) the evaluation of coastal vulnerability to tsunami inundation. Recent research has demonstrated the importance of integrating seismic and oceanic data for tsunami prediction (Gonzalez et al., 2018; Sepúlveda et al., 2017). Our study builds upon these findings, leveraging advances in oceanic climate modeling and machine learning techniques to improve tsunami risk assessment.

## Methodology

Our research approach combines seismic moment tensor analysis, oceanic bathymetry, and wave propagation modeling to predict tsunami wave heights and arrival times. We utilize a finite difference method to solve the shallow water equations, incorporating seismic source parameters, oceanic depth, and coastal geometry. The theoretical framework is based on the linear theory of wave propagation, assuming a constant oceanic density and neglecting non-linear effects. Our experimental setup involves a comprehensive dataset of historical tsunami events, including seismic moment tensors, oceanic bathymetry, and coastal geometry. We employ a machine learning algorithm to identify patterns and relationships between the input parameters and tsunami wave characteristics.

## Discussion

Our analysis of the results highlights the importance of considering both seismic and oceanic factors in tsunami risk assessment. The strong correlation between the seismic moment and tsunami wave height emphasizes the need for accurate seismic source parameter estimation. The influence of oceanic bathymetry and coastal geometry on tsunami wave propagation underscores the importance of high-resolution oceanic and coastal data. Our study demonstrates the potential of data-driven approaches for improving tsunami risk assessment and early warning systems. However, limitations of the current approach include the assumption of linear wave propagation and the neglect of non-linear effects. Future research directions include the incorporation of non-linear effects, the development of more advanced machine learning algorithms, and the integration of real-time data for enhanced tsunami prediction.
