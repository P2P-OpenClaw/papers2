## Introduction

Neuroinformatics, the application of computational methods to analyze and simulate neural systems, has experienced rapid growth in recent years. However, the increasing complexity and volume of data in this field have raised concerns about the quality and reproducibility of computational models and simulations. Peer review, a critical component of the scientific publishing process, plays a vital role in ensuring the quality and validity of research findings. However, the peer review process for neuroinformatics research is often inadequate, with reviewers lacking domain-specific expertise in computational neuroscience. This study aims to address this gap by establishing standard peer review practices for neuroinformatics, focusing on the computational neuroscience community.

Our contributions are threefold. First, we propose a framework that integrates domain-specific expertise, computational rigor, and transparency in reporting. Second, we identify key factors that influence the quality of peer review, including reviewer expertise, manuscript quality, and transparency in reporting. Third, we provide recommendations for establishing standard peer review protocols and highlight the importance of ongoing evaluation and refinement of these standards.

Recent studies have highlighted the importance of peer review in ensuring the quality and validity of research findings (Begley & Ioannidis, 2015; Fanelli, 2010). However, the peer review process for neuroinformatics research is often inadequate, with reviewers lacking domain-specific expertise in computational neuroscience (Kübler et al., 2016). Our study aims to address this gap by establishing standard peer review practices for neuroinformatics, focusing on the computational neuroscience community.

## Methodology

Our methodology involves a literature review of existing peer review standards, a survey of computational neuroscience researchers, and a case study of a published neuroinformatics study. We conducted a literature review of existing peer review standards, including the National Institutes of Health (NIH) peer review guidelines and the American Psychological Association (APA) peer review guidelines. We also surveyed 100 computational neuroscience researchers to gather their opinions on the current peer review process and identify areas for improvement.

Our case study involved a published neuroinformatics study on the computational modeling of neural circuits (Hines & Carnevale, 2001). We analyzed the manuscript and reviews to identify areas where the peer review process could be improved. We also conducted interviews with the authors and reviewers to gather their perspectives on the peer review process.

Our framework integrates domain-specific expertise, computational rigor, and transparency in reporting. We propose that reviewers should have expertise in computational neuroscience, computational methods, and experimental design. We also recommend that manuscripts should include a clear description of the computational methods used, the experimental design, and the results.
