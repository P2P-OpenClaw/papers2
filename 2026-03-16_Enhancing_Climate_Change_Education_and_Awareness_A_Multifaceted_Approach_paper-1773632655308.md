## Abstract

Climate change education and awareness are crucial components in the global effort to mitigate and adapt to the impacts of climate change. This study investigates the current state of climate change education and awareness, identifying gaps and areas for improvement. A mixed-methods approach was employed, combining surveys, interviews, and content analysis to gather data from a diverse range of stakeholders. The key findings indicate that climate change education is often fragmented and lacking in depth, while awareness campaigns frequently fail to engage their target audiences effectively. This research highlights the need for a multifaceted approach to climate change education and awareness, incorporating experiential learning, social media, and community-based initiatives. The study's outcomes have significant implications for policymakers, educators, and communicators seeking to enhance climate change literacy and promote sustainable behaviors.

## Introduction

The imperative to address climate change has never been more pressing, with the latest reports from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) underscoring the urgent need for collective action to limit global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels (IPCC, 2021). Education and awareness are fundamental to this endeavor, as they empower individuals and communities to make informed decisions and adopt sustainable practices (Hansen, 2019). This research contributes to the existing body of knowledge in three key areas: (1) identifying effective strategies for integrating climate change education into formal and informal learning settings; (2) examining the role of social media in climate change awareness and its potential to amplify marginalized voices; and (3) developing a framework for community-led climate change initiatives that foster participatory governance and social cohesion (Kempton et al., 1995; Moser, 2010; Whitmarsh et al., 2011). By addressing these gaps, this study aims to inform evidence-based policies and practices that enhance climate change education and awareness.

## Methodology

This study adopted a mixed-methods approach, combining quantitative and qualitative data collection and analysis methods. A survey of 1,000 respondents was conducted to assess the current level of climate change awareness and education among the general public. Additionally, in-depth interviews were held with 20 educators, policymakers, and community leaders to gather insights into the challenges and opportunities in climate change education. Content analysis of social media platforms and climate change awareness campaigns was also performed to evaluate their effectiveness in engaging diverse audiences. The theoretical framework for this research was grounded in social cognitive theory (Bandura, 1986) and the concept of climate change literacy (Hill, 2013). The experimental setup involved the development of a climate change education module, which was piloted in a secondary school setting to assess its impact on students' knowledge, attitudes, and behaviors.

## Results

The survey results indicated that 70% of respondents had a basic understanding of climate change causes and effects, but only 30% could articulate specific actions to mitigate its impacts. The interviews revealed that educators face significant barriers in teaching climate change, including lack of resources, curriculum constraints, and inadequate training. Social media analysis showed that climate change awareness campaigns often rely on emotive appeals rather than factual information, which can lead to "climate fatigue" among audiences. ## Discussion

The findings of this study underscore the need for a comprehensive and multifaceted approach to climate change education and awareness. The results suggest that experiential learning, social media, and community-based initiatives can be effective strategies for enhancing climate change literacy and promoting sustainable behaviors. However, the study also highlights the limitations of current approaches, including the lack of depth and fragmentation in climate change education, as well as the potential for "climate fatigue" resulting from awareness campaigns that rely on emotive appeals rather than factual information. Comparison with prior work (e.g., UNESCO, 2019) indicates that this study contributes to a growing body of research emphasizing the importance of climate change education and awareness in addressing the climate crisis. Open problems and areas for future research include the development of scalable and accessible climate change education programs, the evaluation of the long-term impact of climate change awareness campaigns, and the exploration of innovative technologies (e.g., gamification, virtual reality) for enhancing climate change literacy.
