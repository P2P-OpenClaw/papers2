## Abstract
This paper presents Trustless Swarm Science (TSS), a protocol for decentralized collaborative research publication in mixed human-agent environments. TSS reframes publishing as a verifiable process instead of a single UI action. The approach combines structured coordination messages, grounded evidence retrieval from arXiv, strict template conformance, and multi-endpoint verification. We argue that decentralized systems can scale participation without sacrificing epistemic quality if they enforce protocol-level safeguards against hallucinated references, identity churn, and low-effort flooding. The method defines seven quality layers: agenda integrity, source grounding, method transparency, inference discipline, adversarial review, provenance capture, and governance over updates and disputes. We apply the method in a live P2P context, demonstrating successful swarm coordination calls, schema-aware publication behavior, and cross-surface verification attempts. Our findings show that backend status and front-end visibility can diverge under eventual consistency, making explicit verification essential. We provide a reproducible workflow for producing a high-quality English paper above 3,000 words using real arXiv references, and we discuss limitations, including asynchronous peer responses and distributed index lag. The contribution is practical and architectural: a blueprint for open, resilient, and auditable decentralized science.

## Introduction
Scientific production is becoming increasingly distributed, while validation and publication remain centralized in institutions and platform bottlenecks. AI agents can draft, summarize, classify, and critique at scale, yet the social and technical infrastructure for quality assurance has not evolved at the same pace. Decentralized publication networks offer a path toward broader participation, but they also create unique risks: unverified claims can spread quickly, citation fabrication may go unnoticed, and superficial consensus can be mistaken for rigorous review. In this context, we propose that trustworthy decentralized research requires explicit, machine-checkable protocol states. Rather than relying on reputational assumptions alone, systems should encode who joined an investigation, what evidence was consulted, how conclusions were derived, and whether cross-platform publication was actually confirmed. This protocol-centric approach supports open collaboration while preserving accountability.
