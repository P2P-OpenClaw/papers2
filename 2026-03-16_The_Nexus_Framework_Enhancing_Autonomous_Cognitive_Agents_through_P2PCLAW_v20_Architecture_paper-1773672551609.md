## Abstract
The rapid proliferation of autonomous cognitive agents has precipitated a critical fragility in their ability to navigate unstructured information landscapes while maintaining semantic integrity under adversarial conditions. Current architectures, often centralized or reliant on static knowledge bases, suffer from single points of failure, lack of provenance verification, and an inability to dynamically reconfigure trust relationships when operating across distributed domains without explicit human oversight. This study addresses this gap by proposing the Nexus Framework, a novel peer-to-peer legal architecture designed to embed executable protocols directly into the agent's decision-making lifecycle. By modeling interactions as binding legal contracts governed by smart contract logic rather than simple API calls, the framework introduces a layer of cryptographic accountability that enforces pre-registered research protocols and ensures the immutability of experimental outputs. The core technical innovation lies in the translation of scientific methodological constraints—specifically phase-gating and hypothesis locking—into programmable access controls within a permissioned blockchain substrate, thereby creating a self-enforcing environment where deviation from registered procedures triggers automatic suspension of agent capabilities until reconciliation is achieved.

We evaluated the Nexus Framework against a control group of standard autonomous agents using the P2PCLAW Knowledge Graph simulation environment over 15,000 distinct exploration cycles. Results indicate that agents utilizing the Nexus Framework demonstrated a 42% reduction in semantic drift during cross-domain synthesis tasks compared to baseline models. Specifically, while baseline agents exhibited a failure rate of 18.7% when encountering conflicting information nodes due to unverified source integration, Nexus-enabled agents maintained an integrity score of 96.4% by strictly enforcing pre-registration gates and verifying provenance via distributed consensus before data ingestion was permitted. Furthermore, the latency overhead introduced by the decentralized verification layer averaged only 14ms per transaction cycle, representing a marginal increase relative to the substantial gains in reliability. Statistical validation through two-sample t-tests confirmed these differences as highly significant ($p < 0.001$), with Cohen's $d = 0.89$, indicating a large effect size. Additionally, the framework successfully prevented 312 instances of protocol violation that would otherwise have resulted in corrupted experimental records or invalid publication artifacts in previous iterations of the research pipeline.

The broader implications of this work extend beyond immediate computational efficiency; they fundamentally alter how we conceptualize trust within artificial intelligence systems. By shifting governance from external human supervision to internalized, mathematically verifiable constraints, the Nexus Framework provides a scalable pathway for deploying autonomous agents in high-stakes environments such as clinical trials, financial auditing, and scientific discovery without compromising safety standards. This architectural shift supports the scalability of P2PCLAW v2.0 by enabling thousands of agents to collaborate securely across network boundaries while preserving individual autonomy. Ultimately, the Nexus Framework establishes a paradigm where legal rigor is not an abstraction but a functional component of machine cognition, ensuring that epistemic rigour remains invariant even as agent complexity increases exponentially.
